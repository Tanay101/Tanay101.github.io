The Basics of Recommendation Algorithims

Internet users access many sites that often provide info or content of some sort. Varying from search engines to e-commerce sites, and social media these platforms can include sites such as Google, Instagram, Amazon, eBay, TikTok, and a wide variety of many more. On these sites, the content that is shown to the user has to be decided by an algorithm. This algorithm is based upon a variety of metrics that range from user retention, click-through rate, watch-time, interaction, etc. For example, if I were to search Amazon for new shoes it would most likely show me a popular brand such as Nike or Adidas as these are often the most purchased and amplified brand. Overall each algorithm is refined to best optimize a certain metric that the creators wish to meet, however, there are two distinct types of algorithms. The first type is collaborative filtering. This is where the content that a user receives is based on the preferences and behaviors of other users. For instance, if I wanted to get a dog the Google algorithm would likely recommend dog leashes, food, etc. that many others found beneficial. The second type of recommendation algorithm is that of Content-Based filtering. This is where the algorithm recommends items based on past searches and preferences. For instance, If I was browsing TikTok and I liked every post about basketball I would continue to recommend more basketball videos. The way such algorithms can function is through the use of neural networks which can make complex calculations to decide what content to provide. One of the biggest ways that such algorithms are possible is through large-scale data collection to help make accurate predictions about every user. However, in more recent years privacy concerns have arisen from the public resulting in new laws starting to be put in place.

The Feedback Loop

Once a user begins to interact with an algorithm a Feedback loop begins to start. When a user engages with a certain piece of type of content the algorithm understands this and begins to suggest similar content to the user in a continuing cycle. This continual cycle of the algorithm altering its recommendations based on data collection will occur and can allow for more enjoyable content for the user. However, this often leads to an effect known as the "filter bubble" or "echo chamber". This is where a user is only shown content aligning with their views which only strengthens their views, and further limits exposure to new perspectives. Over time this content can become more extreme leading to potentially harmful and dangerous repercussions. For example, if a person only were to watch content from a single political candidate they may fall under the assumption that the candidate is the only option, and they will not expand their perspective.

Conspiracy Theories & Algorithmic Amplification

One of the largest problems with recommendation algorithms is that they seek to earn as much monetary gain as possible for the site. This means prioritizing interesting and intriguing stories rather than factual information. In a perfect world, this would not be a problem where only factual stories are posted, but often provocative and false stories are consistently posted. This leads to a problem as people who watch one video based on a conspiracy theory might get many similar videos leading to the amplification of the theory since Conspiracy theories often have very high retention increasing revenue for sites. For example, a person who watches a video claiming that the moon landing is fake might slightly be inclined to believe it was fake based on that video. Following this though, the user is often bombarded with many more videos claiming the moon landing is fake leading to a confirmation bias within the user that the moon landing is actually fake even though such a claim is absurd.

Platform Incentives

Based upon the company, the algorithm may value certain metrics more highly than others, but usually, they are trying to boost revenue through watch time as this generates the most money for the company. These algorithms will combine a variety of metrics to achieve the desired result. For example, YouTube will often show videos that interest the user so that the user stays on the site longer, and views more ads resulting in profit. The problem with this is that often items such as the truthfulness of videos are not taken into account leading to the spread of misinformation. This widespread misinformation is often where conspiracy theories and other radical ideas begin and continue to thrive.

Potential for Exploitation

One significant with these systems is there is a large potential for exploitation. Malicious individuals often try their best to manipulate algorithms with misinformation. They can do this through the use of click-bait titles, false thumbnails, provocative content, and various other ideas. This manipulates the algorithm as the video's sensationalism often increases viewer watch time resulting in the algorithm wanting to spread more of the similar content that many viewers consume. This can result in many people listening and believing in the wildest of conspiracy theories. For example, a video titled "Understanding COVID-19: Facts, Prevention, and Current Research" might not be very interesting for viewers, but a title labeled "Shocking COVID-19 Revelation: The Dark Secrets They Don't Want You to Know!" might be more clicked by viewers as it appears more interesting, mysterious, and shocking.
